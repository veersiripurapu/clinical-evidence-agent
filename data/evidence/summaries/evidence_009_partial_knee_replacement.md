## User-authored summary

For patients whose osteoarthritis is confined to one compartment of the knee, partial
(unicompartmental) knee replacement is an alternative to total knee replacement, and
the evidence describes a genuine tradeoff rather than a clear winner. Compared with
total replacement, partial replacement is associated with faster recovery, shorter
hospital stay, fewer short-term complications, better reported knee function and
quality of life, and lower mortality and thromboembolic risk. However, it carries a
notably higher revision rate — roughly two to three times that of total replacement at
around five years in several analyses. Both are described as viable options for
isolated unicompartmental disease, and the choice depends on the pattern of disease,
patient factors, priorities, and surgeon experience. Outcomes also appear to vary with
how frequently a centre performs the procedure.

## Useful for agent

- Presenting more than one surgical option, rather than treating "surgery" as monolithic.
- Demonstrating honest handling of a true tradeoff (better function vs. higher revision risk).
- Supporting shared decision-making framing between clinician and patient.

## Claims NOT allowed from this source

- Do not present revision-rate figures as this patient's personal risk.
- Do not claim one procedure is universally better than the other.
