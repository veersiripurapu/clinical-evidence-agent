## User-authored summary

For overweight and obese adults with knee osteoarthritis, weight loss is an
evidence-supported non-surgical treatment, and the benefit appears dose-dependent.
Trial evidence indicates that a reduction of at least about 5% of body weight is
associated with improved function, that around 10% loss is associated with greater
reductions in pain and substantially improved function, and that in one NIH-funded
randomized trial analysis, losses of 20% or more were associated with further
improvement in pain, function, and physical quality of life compared with 10%.
Diet combined with exercise has been among the more effective approaches studied.
Individual results vary, and achieving and sustaining weight loss is itself difficult.

## Useful for agent

- Presenting weight loss as a treatment option, not only as a surgical risk factor.
- Explaining that the benefit scales with the amount of weight lost.
- Supporting conservative-management discussion for patients with overweight/obesity.
