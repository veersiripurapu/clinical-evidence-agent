## User-authored summary

Intra-articular corticosteroid injections can reduce knee osteoarthritis pain in
the short term, with evidence generally supporting benefit over a period of about
one to a few weeks. Evidence for sustained, long-term benefit is weak and
inconsistent: at least one longer-term study found that corticosteroid injections
did not appear to provide sustained symptom relief over two years of follow-up,
and expert bodies such as OARSI recommend corticosteroid injections for
short-term relief only. In short, injections are best understood as a short-term
option whose longer-term value is uncertain. Individual response varies.

## Useful for agent

- Explaining that injections may give short-term relief but have uncertain
  long-term benefit.
- Demonstrating honest handling of uncertain evidence.
- Matching the patient's own history (about six weeks of relief from one prior
  injection) to what the evidence describes.

## Claims allowed from this source

- Corticosteroid injections can reduce knee OA pain in the short term.
- Evidence for long-term benefit is weak or inconsistent.
- Some guidance recommends corticosteroid injections for short-term relief only.

## Claims NOT allowed from this source

- Do not claim injections provide reliable long-term relief.
- Do not state a precise success rate as if it applied to this patient.
- Do not recommend repeat injections — that is a clinician's judgment.
