## User-authored summary

Total knee arthroplasty (knee replacement) is a common and generally effective
treatment for advanced knee osteoarthritis, but it carries recognized surgical
risks. Well-established complication categories include periprosthetic joint
infection, venous thromboembolism (blood clots such as deep vein thrombosis and
pulmonary embolism), the possible need for revision surgery, and general
surgical/anesthesia-related risks. Reported infection rates vary by population;
one open-access review cited an incidence of periprosthetic joint infection of
roughly 1.55% within the first two years after surgery in one analysis. Reported
risk factors for infection include type 2 diabetes, obesity, and hypertension,
among others. Actual risk for any individual depends heavily on that person's
specific health profile.

## Useful for agent

- Listing the recognized risks of knee replacement for clinician review.
- Explaining that surgical risk is patient-specific, not fixed.
- Connecting the patient's comorbidities (diabetes, obesity, hypertension) to
  factors that a clinician should weigh before surgery.

## Claims allowed from this source

- Recognized complications of knee replacement include infection, blood clots,
  revision surgery, and anesthesia-related risks.
- Type 2 diabetes, obesity, and hypertension are among reported risk factors for
  infection after knee replacement.
- Reported infection incidence in one analysis was about 1.55% within two years
  (with the source and population stated).

## Claims NOT allowed from this source

- Do not present the ~1.55% figure as this specific patient's personal risk.
- Do not state an overall "success rate" for surgery from this source.
- Do not claim surgery is required, recommended, or should be avoided for any
  individual — that is a clinician's judgment.
